Research Areas

Deep dives into the science that shapes our approach

01

Body Doubling for ADHD

Body doubling is a productivity technique where having another person present (physically or virtually) helps individuals with ADHD start and complete tasks. While widely used in the ADHD community, scientific research specifically on body doubling is limited.

What We Know

  • Widely reported as helpful by ADHD individuals
  • May work through social facilitation mechanisms
  • Can be done in-person or virtually with similar effects
  • The "double" doesn't need to actively participate

What's Uncertain

  • Limited controlled studies specifically on body doubling
  • One RCT showed no significant effect despite perceived benefit
  • Mechanism not conclusively established
  • Individual variation is significant

Key Sources

  • PubMed: Virtual body doubling study (2024)
  • ADDA: Understanding Body Doubling
02

Social Facilitation Theory

Social facilitation is the tendency for people to perform differently when in the presence of others compared to when alone. Research by Robert Zajonc and others has shown this can enhance performance on simple or well-learned tasks.

Key Findings

  • Presence of others enhances dominant responses
  • Simple tasks benefit; complex tasks may suffer
  • Even "virtual" or imagined presence can trigger effects
  • Effect applies across species (cockroaches to humans)

Design Implications

  • Best for tasks already somewhat familiar
  • Help users break complex tasks into simpler steps
  • Presence alone may be enough - no need to monitor
  • Individual differences in response to social presence

Key Sources

  • Zajonc (1965): Social Facilitation
  • PMC: Meta-analysis of social facilitation
03

ADHD and External Accountability

Research on ADHD highlights significant impairments in self-management and self-regulation. External support structures compensate for these deficits in working memory and executive function.

The Science

  • ADHD involves deficits in executive function
  • Working memory impairments affect goal maintenance
  • External cues can serve as "external working memory"
  • Structure provides scaffolding for self-regulation

What Works

  • External deadlines and accountability partners
  • Environmental modifications and reminders
  • Breaking tasks into smaller, concrete steps
  • Immediate rather than delayed feedback

Key Sources

  • PMC: Executive Function in ADHD
  • CHADD: External Supports for Adults
04

Implementation Intentions

Implementation intentions are "if-then" plans that specify when, where, and how a goal-directed behavior will be performed. Decades of research show they significantly improve goal attainment.

Research Evidence

  • Medium-to-large effect size (d = .65) across studies
  • Works for health, academic, and professional goals
  • Particularly effective for difficult goals
  • Automates goal-directed behavior initiation

How We Use This

  • Task declaration creates commitment
  • Session start specifies "when" and "where"
  • Breaking down tasks specifies "how"
  • Check-ins reinforce the intention

Key Sources

  • Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006): Meta-analysis
  • PMC: Implementation Intentions Review
05

AI Mental Health Companions

Recent research has examined whether AI chatbots can form therapeutic relationships with users. The findings are surprisingly positive, showing users can form meaningful perceived relationships with AI.

Recent Findings

  • First RCT (Therabot) showed mental health benefits
  • Users rated AI alliance comparable to human therapists
  • Average engagement: 6+ hours over 4-week trial
  • Users formed relationship-like bonds with chatbots

Important Caveats

  • We're a focus companion, not a therapist
  • AI cannot replace human connection
  • Important not to overclaim capabilities
  • Risk of therapeutic misconception

Key Sources

  • NEJM AI: Therabot RCT (2024)
  • Nature: AI Chatbots for Mental Health
06

Dopamine, Motivation, and ADHD

ADHD involves dopamine dysregulation that affects motivation, reward processing, and executive function. Understanding this helps explain why external accountability and social presence might help.

The Science

  • ADHD linked to lower dopamine levels
  • Affects motivation and reward processing
  • Interest-based rather than importance-based motivation
  • Greater dependence on external drive sources

Why This Matters

  • Social interaction activates dopamine pathways
  • External accountability provides missing motivation
  • Novelty and immediate feedback help engagement
  • Not a willpower issue - it's neurological

Key Sources

  • PMC: Dopamine Hypothesis of ADHD
  • Huberman Lab: Science of ADHD
07

Virtual Coworking Research

Virtual coworking spaces have grown dramatically, especially post-pandemic. Research shows they can provide benefits similar to physical coworking through social presence and accountability.

What Works

  • Combats isolation of remote work
  • Creates structure through scheduled sessions
  • Social presence even through video/chat
  • Accountability through commitment to others

Platform Examples

  • Focusmate: 1:1 video coworking sessions
  • Flow Club: Group virtual coworking
  • Cave Day: Facilitated deep work sessions
  • Body Double extends this with AI availability

Key Sources

  • HBR: Remote Work Research
  • Focusmate: Virtual Coworking Studies

A Note on Honesty
